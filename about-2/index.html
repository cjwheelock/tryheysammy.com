We're trying to reverse a two-decade decline in showing up.

Americans—especially young Americans—spend less time together in person than any generation on record. Hey Sammy exists to bend that line back: one person, one recurring activity, one real place at a time.

What we believe

Loneliness isn't a personal failure. It's a design problem.

And design problems can be fixed.

The quiet collapse of hanging out.

This didn't happen overnight, and it didn't happen because people stopped wanting friends. Over twenty years, the places and habits that once made friendship automatic—school, neighborhoods, clubs, third places—gave way to screens, delivery apps, and bigger, more comfortable homes that quietly ask nothing of us.

Disconnection isn't just sad. It's dangerous.

In 2023, the U.S. Surgeon General declared loneliness a public health epidemic, comparing its mortality impact to smoking up to 15 cigarettes a day. The body treats prolonged disconnection as a threat—and it responds accordingly.

The good news: connection is the strongest medicine we know of.

Every trend above has a mirror image. The same research that documents the harm of isolation documents something more hopeful: few things in medicine protect health, happiness, and longevity as reliably as close relationships—and relationships are buildable.

Friendship has a price, and it's payable

It takes hours—not luck.

Researchers have actually measured how friendship forms. It's not chemistry or charisma. It's accumulated time together—which is exactly why a weekly activity you return to beats a hundred one-off events.

Third places do the math for you.

Sociologist Ray Oldenburg called them third places: spots beyond home and work where people gather informally and become known. A run club, a dance social, a pottery studio, a volunteer shift, a rec league. They work because they bundle everything friendship needs—an activity, a rhythm, familiar faces—into one recurring commitment. And they're closing across the country, which makes finding the ones still thriving matter more, not less.

  • ↳ Something to do, so talking is easy
  • ↳ A schedule, so the hours accumulate
  • ↳ Regulars, so faces become familiar
  • ↳ A skill to learn, so you grow while you belong
  • ↳ A next week, so no single visit carries the pressure

I've lived the statistics—both directions.

During a lost year away from college, I was the numbers above: isolated, aimless, mostly indoors. Then a friend dragged me to a ballroom dance class in Tucson. I kept going back. Strangers taught me for free, stayed late to practice, and eventually handed me a key to the studio. That one recurring commitment led to West Coast Swing, real friendships, and eventually a new life in San Francisco.

I didn't get lucky. I just showed up somewhere repeatedly—and the research says that's most of what it takes. Hey Sammy is my attempt to give more people that same on-ramp: an app that finds recurring, participatory activities near you, tells you exactly what to expect walking in alone, helps you put it on the calendar, and nudges you to go back until the room knows your name.

People help people who show up.

Hey Sammy doesn't try to be the community. The communities already exist, run by organizers who do the real work. We just build the bridge.

How we're pushing back against these trends.

  • Help every user make one recurring real-world commitment—the single highest-leverage change the research supports.
  • Make showing up alone feel doable: what to wear, where to stand, whether beginners are welcome.
  • Get first-timers back for visits two, three, and four—because the hours are what turn faces into friends.
  • Send committed regulars to the local organizers and third places keeping community alive.
  • Help people learn real skills—dancing, throwing pots, running farther—so confidence compounds alongside connection.
  • Measure success in regulars, not downloads.

Built for a public benefit.

Hey Sammy is a registered public benefit corporation. Our stated public benefit is to create media and technology that helps young adults build fulfilling lives. We're starting with a practical part of that mission: helping people find places worth returning to, form close friendships, build real skills, and become part of the communities around them.
